Caring for Your Cat

Cats, initially domesticated around 7500 BCE in western Asia, currently rank as the second most popular domestic pet in the United States, boasting over 60 documented breeds. While care and diet may vary among breeds, some general practices contribute to the well-being of your feline companion.

In the first four weeks of life, it's crucial for the queen (a term for a pregnant or nursing female cat) to nurse her young directly. Monitoring kitten growth is essential, and if any show insufficient weight gain, caretakers may need to provide direct feeding using a bottle or tube. Factors affecting weight gain include competition for milk, gastrointestinal issues, or adverse environmental conditions.

From four weeks to one year, kittens can be introduced to soft wet kitten food, with daily recommended amounts according to the ASPCA, Breed-specific dietary needs may exist, requiring consultation with a veterinarian. As cats transition from one year to seven years, their activity level and metabolism decrease, suggesting scheduled meals over leaving food out all day to prevent obesity.

For cats aged seven years and older, dietary adjustments become crucial. Reduced fats and calories are recommended, emphasizing quality proteins with specific sources like salmon" rather than generic categories like fish," which may indicate lower-quality protein byproducts. This tailored approach addresses the changes in the aging cat's body
